---
title: "Output Is Easy to Count, Impact Is Easy to Miss"
date: 2026-05-28
description: "Commit counts measure motion. The work that moves a team most often leaves the smallest trace."
---

We measure what's easy to measure. Commits, PRs, lines, tickets closed. They're all proxies for motion, and motion is not the same as impact.

Some of the highest-leverage work I've seen leaves almost no trace in those metrics. The architecture pattern that prevents a class of outages from ever happening — it shows up as *fewer* incidents, which no dashboard celebrates. The design review comment that redirects a quarter of effort before a line is written. The SME who unblocks four other engineers in a hallway conversation. The cost optimization that saves a budget but ships as a config change.

The inverse is just as true: it's easy to generate enormous output that moves nothing. Rewrites nobody needed. Abstractions that add indirection without removing decisions. Heroic firefighting on fires you started.

So when I think about impact — my own or someone I'm calibrating — I try to ask a different question. Not "how much did they produce," but "what is true now that wasn't true before, and would it have happened without them?" Did the team get faster? Did a whole category of problem disappear? Did someone else level up because of how this person worked?

That's harder to put in a spreadsheet. It's also the only version of the question worth answering. The best engineers I know optimize for the second-order effects — the things that make the *next* hundred commits cheaper — and they're comfortable that the scoreboard will never quite capture it.
