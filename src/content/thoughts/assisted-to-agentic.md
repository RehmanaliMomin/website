---
title: "From Assisted to Agentic"
date: 2026-05-20
description: "The mental-model shift that mattered more than any single feature — and why the usage meter is the new constraint."
---

For a couple of years my mental model for AI coding tools was *assisted programming*. The tool sat in my editor, finished my lines, suggested the next block. I was still the one writing the program; it was just typing faster than me.

Somewhere recently that model broke. Not because the autocomplete got better — because the unit of work changed. I stopped handing the tool a line to finish and started handing it an *outcome*: investigate this, fix that, verify it, open the PR. The good ones now close the loop themselves. That's not a faster horse. It's a different animal.

The honest version of this shift includes the friction. When your tool runs continuously instead of reactively, you start living against a usage meter — and you feel every change to it. I've watched a generous weekly allowance evaporate "like oil" under real agentic load, and I've been pleasantly surprised when a rolling five-hour window quietly made the caps stop mattering. The constraint moved from *how fast can I type* to *how much agent time can I afford this week* — which is a strange and revealing place for the bottleneck to land.

What I take from it: the leverage is real enough that I reorganized how I work around it, and the economics are still settling. Both things are true. The teams that win won't be the ones with the cleverest prompts; they'll be the ones who rebuilt their workflow for delegation and learned to spend the meter where it actually compounds.
